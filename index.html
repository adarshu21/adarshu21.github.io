Interviews

Building Rapport with the Interviewer

When I first entered the interview, I greeted the interviewer warmly and maintained a positive and confident demeanor. I expressed my enthusiasm for the opportunity to interview with Oracle, mentioning my admiration for the company’s long-standing reputation in the tech industry. To establish a connection, I asked the interviewer about their role at Oracle and how they have enjoyed working there. This created a friendly atmosphere and showed my genuine interest in the company culture.

Explaining Internships and Projects Thoroughly

I began by summarizing my most recent internship experience, focusing on how it developed my skills relevant to the role at Oracle. For instance, I worked on a cloud computing project where I designed and implemented scalable cloud-based solutions, which aligns with Oracle’s cloud services. I highlighted specific technologies used, such as Firebase and SQL schemas, and discussed challenges I overcame, demonstrating problem-solving abilities. I also described key projects, emphasizing their relevance to the role. For example, I explained how my experience with multithreading applications would help me contribute to Oracle’s software development processes.

Aligning with Oracle’s Values, Mission, and Goals

Before the interview, I researched Oracle’s mission, vision, and recent initiatives, such as their focus on cloud services, data management, and enterprise software solutions. During the interview, I aligned my experiences and career goals with Oracle’s mission. For example, I mentioned how my projects fostered a culture of innovation, which I aim to bring to Oracle. I tailored my responses to show how my skills and experiences would directly contribute to Oracle’s goals, such as improving software efficiency and enhancing customer satisfaction.

Round 1: Technical Deep Dive

  • Check BT Balancing: I solved the binary tree balancing problem, explained all the complexities, and optimized the solution.
  • Multithreading, implementation: I explained how I implemented multithreading in a previous project, ensuring thread safety and efficient resource management. Key concepts included synchronization, deadlock avoidance, and performance optimization.
  • Firebase, cloud: I described how I used Firebase for real-time data synchronization and its advantages in cloud-based applications. Key concepts included cloud architecture and data security.
  • Java OOPs brush up with implementation and writeup: I provided a brief explanation of key OOP principles like inheritance, polymorphism, and encapsulation, followed by a code example. Key concepts included OOP principles and design patterns.
  • SQL Schema Design: During the interview, I was asked to design a relational database schema. I took this opportunity to explain the database schema I designed for a hackathon project focused on a Pharmaceutical Management System. I began by outlining the core requirements of the system, such as managing inventory, tracking prescriptions, and handling supplier information. I then described how I identified the key entities, including Patients, Doctors, Medicines, Suppliers, and Prescriptions, and their relationships. I explained the normalization process I followed to ensure data integrity and avoid redundancy, ensuring the schema was in Third Normal Form (3NF). To demonstrate my approach, I drew the Entity-Relationship (ER) diagram and detailed how I implemented the schema in SQL. Additionally, I discussed how I optimized the schema for efficient querying, particularly focusing on JOIN operations and indexing strategies. The interviewer appreciated the practical application of my skills and the way I tailored the schema to meet the specific needs of the project.
  • System Design Skills: When asked about my system design skills, I shared my experience from a project I worked on at JPMorgan, where I was responsible for designing a complex financial application from scratch. I explained how I approached the project by breaking it down into parts, subcomponents, components, subsystems, and finally, the system level. This methodical approach ensured that each aspect of the system was well-integrated and scalable. Additionally, I mentioned that I had taken an elective in System Engineering, which provided me with a solid foundation in designing and analyzing systems at various levels. I explained how this knowledge allowed me to design robust, efficient systems that could handle the scale and complexity required in real-world applications. The interviewer seemed impressed with my ability to apply theoretical knowledge from my coursework to practical, industry-relevant projects.
  • Explaining Projects: After discussing the technical questions, the interviewer asked me to explain my previous projects. I began by providing an overview of each project, focusing on the technologies used, the challenges faced, and the impact of the work. For example, I talked about a project where I implemented a federated learning model at JP Morgan, which involved decentralized machine learning across different devices to enhance data privacy. I also discussed my work with HPCC, where I developed a scalable NLP parser for English Wikipedia entries, showcasing how I optimized the parsing process on a distributed platform. Additionally, I shared details about my internship at DRDO, where I applied deep learning techniques to solve complex problems in defense technology. Throughout the explanations, I made sure to connect the skills and technologies I used in these projects to the requirements of the role at Oracle, emphasizing how my experience would be valuable in contributing to their team.

Round 2: System Design and Big Data

  • Load Balancing Types and Real-Life Scenario: The interviewer presented a real-life scenario involving a high-traffic web application and asked how I would implement load balancing to ensure the system's availability and performance. I began by explaining the different types of load balancing strategies, including round-robin, least connections, and IP hash, and how each one is suited to different types of workloads. I then introduced the CAP theorem, which highlights the trade-offs between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance in distributed systems, and discussed how these principles affect the choice of load balancing strategy. Following this, I explained the architectures of various distributed systems, including master-slave, peer-to-peer, and microservices architectures, and how load balancing fits into each. To address the specific scenario, I proposed an alternative solution called priority load balancing, where traffic is routed based on the priority of the request, optimizing for critical tasks first. I related this approach to network routing and optimization techniques, drawing parallels to how routers prioritize packets in a network. The interviewer appreciated the depth of my explanation and the innovative approach I proposed, which demonstrated my ability to think critically and adapt solutions to real-world challenges.
  • DevOps Basics: The interviewer then asked about my understanding of DevOps. I began by explaining the core principles of DevOps, including continuous integration (CI), continuous delivery (CD), and the importance of automation in the software development lifecycle. I discussed the benefits of using DevOps practices, such as faster release cycles, improved collaboration between development and operations teams, and more reliable deployments.
  • High-Performance Computing (HPC) and Distributed Systems: I then expanded on my experience with High-Performance Computing (HPC) and distributed systems, particularly with the HPCC (High-Performance Computing Cluster) platform. I shared my experience working on a project where I developed a scalable natural language processing (NLP) parser for English Wikipedia entries on the HPCC Systems Big Data Platform. This project involved processing large datasets efficiently by leveraging distributed computing resources. I explained how I utilized HPCC’s Thor and Roxie clusters for data-intensive tasks, with Thor handling massive data transformations and Roxie providing real-time data delivery for analytical applications. Additionally, I mentioned my paper presentation in Japan, titled “Scalable Analysis of English Dictionary Files on HPCC Systems Big Data Platform with Apache, Hadoop, and Big Data.” In the paper, I compared the performance of HPCC with Hadoop, focusing on the scalability and efficiency of MapReduce operations in handling big data. I highlighted the key differences between the two platforms, such as HPCC’s schema-less data handling versus Hadoop’s structured data processing, and discussed how each platform is suited for different types of big data applications. The interviewer was particularly interested in this discussion, as it demonstrated my deep understanding of distributed systems and my ability to apply this knowledge in practical, research-driven projects.
  • Scalability and Its Types: The final part of our discussion focused on scalability, a crucial aspect of system design. I explained the two primary types of scalability: horizontal and vertical scaling. Horizontal scaling, also known as scaling out, involves adding more machines or nodes to a system, which allows for the distribution of load across multiple servers. This approach is often used in distributed systems and cloud environments where elasticity is required to handle varying workloads. Vertical scaling, or scaling up, involves increasing the capacity of a single machine by adding more resources such as CPU, memory, or storage. I discussed the benefits and limitations of each approach, emphasizing that the choice between them depends on the specific requirements of the application, such as cost, complexity, and performance needs. I also explained how these scalability strategies can be implemented in cloud environments, using auto-scaling groups and load balancers to dynamically adjust resources based on demand. The interviewer seemed pleased with my thorough understanding of scalability and ended the round on a positive note. Although he did not provide specific feedback, his supportive demeanor indicated that he was satisfied with my answers.
  • DRDO Project - Target Recognition and Semantic Segmentation: I also discussed my internship project at LRDE, DRDO, where I worked on target recognition and semantic segmentation for SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar) images of military vehicles. This project involved developing and training deep learning models tailored to the unique challenges of Indian terrain. I utilized advanced models like Roboflow-SegFormer and U-Net (CNN) to achieve high accuracy in segmenting and identifying targets within Umbra-SAR images. I explained how I approached the task by first pre-processing the SAR images to enhance feature extraction and then iteratively refining the model to improve its performance on complex terrain features. The interviewer was particularly interested in how these models could be applied to real-world defense scenarios, and I detailed how the techniques I developed could contribute to more accurate and reliable target recognition in various environmental conditions.

Round 3: Live Coding and HR Questions

  • Live Coding: After a brief break, I was called for Round 3. I explained all my internships and projects, including work on JP Morgan's federated learning (decentralized machine learning), HPCC (distributed platform and scalable NLP parsing on English Wikipedia entries), and my deep learning internship at DRDO. The interviewer then handed me a laptop and asked me to code a solution within 5 minutes. The task was to reverse a linked list, which I quickly implemented in Java using a 3-pointer approach. Meanwhile, he asked why I wanted to join Oracle, whether I was sure about not continuing with other internships, and if I really wanted to work at Oracle. I explained that I had no plans for a master’s degree and that my vision, mission, and values align with those of the company. I also expressed how I could add value to Oracle. Just as I was about to compile the code, the 5 minutes were up.
  • Feedback and Role Discussion: I again asked for feedback, but the interviewer didn’t provide much. I inquired about the roles and responsibilities, and he explained the different verticals at Oracle. The discussion ended on a positive note, with the interviewer seeming satisfied with my performance.

Round 4: Final Discussion with HR

After the technical rounds, I had a casual discussion with the HR representative, covering topics like citizenship, job description, worksite, and flexibility. The conversation was straightforward, and the HR seemed pleased with my responses.

Verdict

Successfully cleared all rounds and received an offer for the role of Associate Software Engineer / Developer at Oracle.

Final Thoughts and Reflections

Reflecting on the interview experience, I learned the importance of preparation, understanding the company’s values, and staying calm under pressure. This experience reinforced my belief in aligning my skills with the company’s goals and being confident in my abilities.

Tips and Resources

Tips for Cracking the Interview

  • Practice DSA Regularly: Focus on practicing Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA) consistently. Use resources like the Striver SDE Sheet to cover all essential topics.
  • Understand the Fundamentals: Be strong in core subjects like DBMS, Operating Systems, Computer Networks, and OOPs. These are often the backbone of technical interviews.
  • Mock Interviews: Take mock interviews to simulate the interview environment. This will help you get comfortable with the pressure and time constraints.
  • Communicate Clearly: Always explain your thought process clearly while solving problems during the interview. This helps the interviewer understand your approach and problem-solving skills.
  • Stay Calm and Confident: It’s natural to feel nervous, but staying calm and confident can help you think clearly and perform better during interviews.
  • Ask for Feedback: Don’t hesitate to ask for feedback after each round. This shows that you are eager to learn and improve, which is a positive trait.
  • Research the Company: Understand the company’s values, mission, and the technologies they work with. Tailor your answers to align with what they are looking for in a candidate.
